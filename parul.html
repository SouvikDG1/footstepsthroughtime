Parul Village’s Bishalakshi Temple: Legend, History, and Terracotta Art

Published on June 11, 2026 • Written by Souvik Dasgupta • Field Journal #42

Among Bengal’s comparatively later terracotta temples stands the Bishalakshi Temple of Parul village in Hooghly district. Dedicated to the all-seeing Goddess Bishalakshi, this south-facing temple, built on a high plinth in the traditional atchala style, is associated with a popular local legend.

Photo 1: The overarching majestic south-facing structure of the historic Bishalakshi Temple at Parul village.

The Legend & Oral History

According to local tradition, the village landlord Raja Ranjit Ray once received a divine vision in which the Goddess promised to be born as his daughter. However, she warned that if he ever asked her to leave, she would depart forever. A few months later, a daughter was indeed born to the landlord’s family. On the day of a Baruni festival, the young girl requested permission to go bathing in the village pond with her friends. Distracted and absent-minded, Ranjit Ray granted her request, and at that very moment the divine child disappeared forever.

While this story belongs to the realm of folklore and local belief, a plaque within the temple premises provides concise historical information about the shrine. The following is an English translation of the inscription:

Temple Introduction This historic temple of Sri Sri Bishalakshi Mata, situated at Bikrampur in the Arambagh subdivision and revered for centuries, is dedicated to the worship of the Goddess Bishalakshi, the presiding deity of the renowned Raja Ranjit Ray and the family deity (Kuladevi) of the Ray family of Madhabpur, descendants of Raja Ranjit Ray. It was at this very site that Raja Ranjit practised Tantric worship of the Goddess. The story of Goddess Bishalakshmi, an incarnation of Durga, taking birth as Raja Ranjit’s daughter has been mentioned for generations in the history of Rarh Bengal, various historical documents, and scholarly articles. The miraculous event is also referred to in Sri Sri Ramakrishna Kathamrita. The present temple was constructed in 1859 CE (1781 Saka Era / 1266 Bengali Era) by the sons of the distinguished landlord Sri Shantiram Ray, founder of the Madhabpur Ray family. The Madhabpur Ray family continues to serve as the hereditary custodians and worshippers of the Goddess. The temple is mentioned in Late Mediaeval Temples of Bengal by David J. McCutchion (Asiatic Society of Bengal, 1972 edition) and also in Narendra Nath Bhattacharya’s work on the antiquities of Hooghly district.

Built on a high plinth and facing south, the temple follows the nineteenth-century Medinipur style of temple architecture and is constructed in the atchala form. The entrance consists of three arched openings.

Intricate Terracotta Art Details

The temple stands on an elevated plinth. Terracotta ornamentation survives only on the façade. Although not extremely old, the terracotta work is elegant and of considerable artistic merit. The temple remains under the management of a private trust. Repeated coats of paint applied over the years have somewhat obscured and damaged the original colour and texture of the terracotta panels. The finial (kalasa) atop the temple tower is particularly striking.

Inside the sanctum is a sacred stone representing Goddess Bishalakshi. Daily worship is performed twice a day. The terracotta panels depict scenes from the battle between Rama and Ravana, human figures, floral motifs, the Samudra Manthana (Churning of the Ocean), and episodes of devotional congregational singing. Circular floral decorations above the arches resemble the well-known “Tree of Life” motif. Like many temples of Bengal, the shrine features a triple-arched (tri-khilan) entrance.

Photo 4: View of the double-tiered Atchala curved roof layout against the skyline.

Daily worship is conducted at the temple. While the exact morning worship schedule is not known, the sanctum generally closes by noon. It reopens around 5:30–6:00 PM for evening worship. The temple premises remain open from morning until approximately 8:00 PM.

Photo 5: The primary entry archway displaying miniature panel framing styles.

Parul village can be easily reached by toto (electric rickshaw) from Arambagh railway station. Visitors should also explore the nearby cremation-ground Kali Temple and Shiva Temple, as well as the ancient Raghunandan Jiu Temple located about five minutes away on foot.
